Physics of a Turning Boat

Coordinate Definitions

Consider a boat moving in a 2-D, cartesian coordinate system. In that system we define the boat heading vector as a unit vector originating at the boat centre of gravity, running along the boat axis (i.e. the axis connecting the boat's stern with the bow) and pointing towards the boat's bow. Then, we can define the following 2 angles:

α: the angle between the x-axis and the boat heading vector.
θ: the angle between the x-axis and the boat velocity vector.

We call the axis along the boat heading vector, the longitudinal axis. All vector quantities along this axis are sufixed by the sufix LON. The axis which is perpendicular to the longitudinal axis is called the lateral axis. Similarly all vector quantitites along this lateral axis are sufixed with the sufix LAT. These 2 axes, along and perpendicular to the boat heading vector, define an alternative coordinate system which is wrt to the boat heading.

Motion Parameters Definition

The boat motion we consider is a free motion, i.e. motion in which the only force acting on the boat is the water resistance. This means that, in this example, no propulsive power exists. The boat is launched with some initial velocity v0, always along its heading vector (i.e. along the longitudinal axis), and then left to move by experiencing only the water resistance force. To describe this motion the following quantities are defined:

v: the magnitude of the boat velocity vector v.
vLON: the boat velocity component along the longitudinal axis.
vLAT: the boat velocity component along the lateral axis.
kLON: the longitudinal water resistance coefficient.
kLAT: the lateral water resistance coefficient.
FLON: the water resistance force component along the longitudinal axis.
FLAT: the water resistance force component along the lateral axis.

We approximate the water resistance force FR experienced by a body moving along the water at a speed v by FR = FR(k, v) where k is the water resistance coefficient and FR(k, v) is:

FR(k, v)
 = {
-kv         : v < vT
-kv2       : v ≥ vT

where vT is some transition velocity denoting the boundary between the high speed and low speed ranges. Please note that this is a rough approximation. By choosing vT = 1 we can make the function continuous (although its first derivative will not be continuous). A better approximation might be to choose separate resistance coefficients for the high and low velocity regions and write a continuous function made up by a combination of the two components, multiplied by sigmoid transition functions around the transition velocity vT.

From the above definition it follows that:

FLON = FR(kLON, vLON)
FLAT = FR(kLAT, vLAT)

Straight Line Motion Description

When a boat is moving freely along some direction in a straight line, it follows that α = θ, i.e. its heading and velocity vectors are aligned. That is a fancy way of saying that the boat's bow is pointing (heading vector) exactly where the boat is actually going (velocity vector). In this case the water resistance force FR is completely aligned along the boat longitudinal axis, and has the efect of slowing the boat down in its straight line cource. Thus we can write:

FLON = FR
FLAT = 0       (since vLAT = 0)

Turning Motion Description

Now we consider the case where the boat is launched with some initial velocity but then it is actually turning (while slowing down). This means that the boat is rotating, i.e. its heading (vector angle) is changing. This is achieved by a rudder or some other means. Obviously, the change of the boat heading will result in the boat itself turning, i.e. its velocity vector changing direction. How does the rotation of the boat make the boat turn? It does because, in this turning motion α ≠ θ. That is, the direction where the boat is pointing (heading vector) is not exactly the same as the direction where the boat is actually going (velocity vector). In this case FLAT ≠ 0. This non-zero, FLAT force, which acts perpendicularly to the boat heading, is the force that turns the boat.

Turning Motion Forces Calculation

Assuming that we know the velocity vector v and the boat heading α, shown above, as well the resistance coefficients kLON and kLAT, we wish to calculate the forces Fx and Fy acting on the turning boat, which are, also, depicted above.

We begin by writing the x and y components of the velocity vector v:

vx = v cos(θ)
vy = v sin(θ)

The rotation matrix R(θ) can be used to transform our coordinate system between the x-y and LON-LAT coordinate systems since we can define one of these coordinates systems as a rotation transformation of the other. The rotation transformation matrix R(θ) is:

R(θ) =
cos(θ) -sin(θ)
sin(θ) cos(θ)

Where the angle θ represents a roation in the clockwise direction.

vLON = vx cos(α) + vy sin(α)
vLAT = -vx sin(α) + vy cos(α)

Substituting for vx and vy we get:

vLON = v cos(θ) cos(α) + v sin(θ) sin(α)
vLAT = -v cos(θ) sin(α) + v sin(θ) cos(α)

So now we have expressions for vLON and vLAT which allows us to directly calculate the resistance forces FLON and FLAT acting directly against and perpendicular to the boat, respectively (i.e. in the LON-LAT coordinate system).

After calculating FLON and FLAT we want to write expressions for Fx and Fy, i.e. the resistance force components in our x-y cordinate system. Fx = FLON cos(α) - FLAT sin(α)

Writing FLON and FLAT in terms of kLON, vLON and kLAT, vLAT respectively gives:

Fx = FR(kLON, vLON) cos(α) - FR(kLAT, vLAT) sin(α)
Fy = FR(kLON, vLON) sin(α) + FR(kLAT, vLAT) cos(α)

Turning Motion Forces Summary

To summarize, the formulae for the forces acting on a turning boat are:

Fx = FR(kLON, vLON) cos(α) - FR(kLAT, vLAT) sin(α)
Fy = FR(kLON, vLON) sin(α) + FR(kLAT, vLAT) cos(α)

where:

vLON = v cos(θ) cos(α) + v sin(θ) sin(α)
vLAT = -v cos(θ) sin(α) + v sin(θ) cos(α)

and the definitions for function FR(k, v), the angles α and θ, the velocity v, as well as the constants kLON and kLAT can be found in the sections Coordinate Definitions and Motion Parameters Definition, at the beginning of this document.
